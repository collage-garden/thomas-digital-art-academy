# Binaural Processing

*How your brainstem invents a beat that doesn't exist in the physical world.*

## The Path from Ear to Perception

Sound enters each ear, vibrates the eardrum, transmits through the ossicles, and reaches the cochlea — a spiral organ that decomposes sound into frequency bands via mechanical resonance along the basilar membrane. Hair cells on the basilar membrane convert vibration into electrical impulses on the auditory nerve.

Up to this point, each ear's signal is processed independently. The two signals first converge at the **Superior Olivary Complex** (SOC) in the brainstem — about 1 cm above the spinal cord, roughly 10 milliseconds after sound onset.

---

## The Superior Olivary Complex

The SOC has two subdivisions critical for binaural processing:

### Medial Superior Olive (MSO)

MSO neurons receive excitatory input from **both** ears. They act as coincidence detectors — they fire maximally when the inputs from left and right ears arrive at precisely the same moment. Because sound from one side reaches the near ear before the far ear, the MSO uses these **Interaural Time Differences** (ITDs) to compute the sound's horizontal direction.

For binaural beats, the MSO performs a different computation: when the left ear receives 400 Hz and the right ear receives 410 Hz, the phase relationship between the two signals continuously rotates. The phase difference cycles from "in phase" to "out of phase" and back 10 times per second. MSO neurons that fire on coincidence will therefore modulate their firing rate at 10 Hz — the beat frequency.

### Lateral Superior Olive (LSO)

LSO neurons compute **Interaural Level Differences** (ILDs) — useful for localizing high-frequency sounds where the head casts an acoustic shadow. For binaural beats, the LSO plays a lesser role since the stimulus is typically moderate and equal in level.

---

## Frequency Following Response (FFR)

The periodic modulation of neural firing in response to the binaural beat can be measured externally as the **Frequency Following Response** — a steady-state evoked potential recorded via EEG electrodes on the scalp.

Key facts:

- The FFR is **objective and reproducible**. It is not a subjective report — it is an electrical signal generated by the brainstem (Pratt et al., 2010).
- It reflects **subcortical** (brainstem) processing, not cortical activity.
- It can be recorded even when the listener is not consciously attending to the beat.

The FFR is a primary piece of evidence that binaural beats are a real neural phenomenon, not an artifact of expectation.

---

## Phase-Locking and Its Limits

The MSO's ability to track phase differences depends on a neural property called **phase-locking**: auditory nerve fibers tend to fire at a particular phase of the stimulus waveform. This is extremely precise at low frequencies — the auditory system can detect ITDs as small as 10 microseconds.

But phase-locking degrades as frequency increases:

- Below ~1500 Hz: strong phase-locking, clear binaural beats
- Above ~1500 Hz: phase-locking weakens — binaural beats become faint
- Beat frequency above ~30–35 Hz: even if the carrier frequencies are low, the rate of phase rotation exceeds the brainstem's tracking ability. The pulsation perception collapses into two separate tones. (Licklider et al., 1950)

The Explorer's **"Collapse Zone"** preset (30 Hz difference) and **"Beyond"** preset (42 Hz) let you experience this boundary directly. The transition from clear pulsation to rough two-tone perception is remarkably abrupt — a vivid demonstration that perception has hard neural limits.

## What Binaural Beats Are NOT

### Not Cortical Brainwave Entrainment

A persistent commercial claim: "10 Hz binaural beats entrain your cortex to alpha waves." This conflates two distinct levels of neural activity:

- **FFR**: generated in the brainstem (SOC), tightly locked to the stimulus, objectively measurable
- **Cortical oscillations** (alpha, theta, etc.): generated by thalamocortical circuits over the entire cortex, reflect global brain state, and are influenced by arousal, attention, and countless other factors

There is no strong evidence that a brainstem FFR at 10 Hz causes the cortex to synchronize at 10 Hz. They are different circuits, different scales, different mechanisms. Systematic reviews (Garcia-Argibay et al., 2019; Orozco Perez et al., 2020) have found weak and inconsistent effects on cortical EEG.

### Not a Therapy

No authoritative medical body (FDA, WHO, NIH) lists binaural beats as a treatment for anxiety, insomnia, ADHD, or any other condition. The subjective relaxation many users report is likely attributable to the **listening context** — eyes closed, quiet environment, headphones isolating external noise — rather than any specific neural effect of the beat frequency.

## Why It Matters for Art

The neuroscience of binaural processing tells us something profound about perception in general:

1. **Your brain constructs experience, it does not passively receive it.** The beat is not in the sound — it is manufactured by the SOC and delivered to consciousness as if it were real.

2. **There are hard limits to what the brain can construct.** Above 35 Hz difference, the illusion breaks. No amount of belief, marketing, or chakra activation changes this.

3. **The subjective experience of "feeling relaxed" or "feeling energized" during binaural beat listening is real — but its cause is ambiguous.** This is precisely the territory explored by the Explorer's satire editions (see [Framing and Placebo](../../knowledge/psychology/framing-and-placebo.md)).
